## Abstract

Autoregressive models for molecular graph generation typically operate on flattened sequences of atoms and bonds, discarding the rich multi-scale structure inherent to molecules. We introduce **MOSAIC** (**M**ulti-scale **O**rganization via **S**tructural **A**bstraction **I**n **C**omposition), a framework that lifts autoregressive generation from flat token walks to compositional, hierarchy-aware sequences. MOSAIC provides a unified three-stage pipeline: (1) **hierarchical coarsening** that recursively groups atoms into motif-like clusters using graph-theoretic methods (spectral clustering, hierarchical agglomerative clustering, and motif-aware variants), (2) **structured tokenization** that serializes the resulting multi-level hierarchy into sequences that explicitly encode parent-child relationships, partition boundaries, and edge connectivity at every level, and (3) **autoregressive generation** with a standard Transformer decoder that learns to produce these structured sequences. We evaluate MOSAIC on the MOSES and COCONUT molecular benchmarks, comparing four tokenization schemes of increasing hierarchical expressiveness. Our experiments show that hierarchy-aware tokenizations improve chemical validity and structural diversity over flat baselines while enabling control over generated substructures. MOSAIC provides a principled, modular foundation for structure-aware molecular generation.

## Introduction

Molecular graphs are a natural representation for drug discovery and property prediction: nodes are atoms, edges are bonds, and validity and function depend heavily on recurring substructures — rings, functional groups, and their connectivity. While discriminative graph neural networks excel at predicting properties from such graphs, *generating* novel, valid molecules remains challenging. Autoregressive transformers, which have scaled successfully in language and code, offer an attractive backbone for graph generation when graphs are first tokenized into sequences. Existing tokenization schemes typically flatten the graph into a single linear order — a random walk or depth-first traversal — so that a causal transformer can predict the next token. These flat walks capture local adjacency and some long-range edges via back-references, but they do not expose the graph's compositional hierarchy: which atoms belong to which ring or functional group, and how those units connect. The model must therefore infer motif structure implicitly from the token stream, which can lead to broken rings, inconsistent motif statistics, and limited control over high-level chemistry.

MOSAIC addresses this gap by introducing an explicit *hierarchical graph* (H-graph) abstraction and a three-stage pipeline for autoregressive molecular generation. First, we *coarsen* the molecular graph into a set of communities and inter-community edges, optionally using domain knowledge (ring and functional-group detection) to guarantee motif cohesion. Second, we *tokenize* the H-graph into a flat sequence using a coarse-before-fine ordering: the model sees community-level structure before atom-level detail, mirroring the compositional nature of chemistry. Third, we *flatten* the generated token sequence back into a molecular graph. All tokenization schemes support a lossless roundtrip from graph to tokens and back.

## Coarsening Strategies

The coarsening step determines how atoms are grouped into communities. We explore two families: *flexible* methods that optimize a graph-theoretic objective without domain knowledge, and *constraint* methods that leverage chemical structure to define communities. **Spectral clustering** uses the eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian to identify natural clusters, selecting the number of partitions that maximizes Newman–Girvan modularity. **Hierarchical agglomerative clustering (HAC)** takes a bottom-up approach, iteratively merging the most similar adjacent nodes based on bond-type-weighted distances, which tends to preserve local chemical neighborhoods. **Motif-aware coarsening (MC)** first identifies known chemical motifs (rings, functional groups) via SMARTS pattern matching, then clusters the remaining atoms with spectral or HAC methods. This ensures that chemically meaningful substructures are preserved as intact units. **MC with functional groups (MC+FG)** extends this with an expanded library of functional-group patterns for finer-grained control over which substructures remain intact during coarsening.

## Tokenization Schemes

Given a coarsened hierarchy, we serialize it into a flat token sequence for autoregressive modeling. A shared design principle across all tokenizers is *coarse-before-fine* ordering: community-level structure always precedes atom-level detail, so the model first commits to the high-level decomposition before filling in local connectivity. **H-SENT** (Hierarchical SENT) encodes multi-level partition structure with explicit partition boundaries and bipartite cross-community edge blocks; each community's atoms are serialized via a SENT walk with back-edge brackets. **HDT** (Hierarchical DFS Tokenization) encodes the full tree structure using depth-first traversal with ENTER/EXIT nesting tokens, capturing both intra- and inter-community edges as back-edges during the DFS, with no separate bipartite reconstruction needed. **HDTC** (HDT with Composition) is the most expressive scheme: it types each community node (ring, functional group, singleton), encodes a super-graph of inter-community bonds, and serializes atom-level detail within each typed community block.

## Results

We evaluate unconditional generation on **MOSES** (~1M drug-like molecules, 10–26 heavy atoms) and **COCONUT** (~5K complex natural products, 30–100 heavy atoms, ≥4 rings). All models use a GPT-2 backbone (12 layers, 768 hidden, 12 heads, ~85M parameters) trained with identical hyperparameters. We generate 500 molecules per model and evaluate against **5,000 randomly sampled references from the combined train+test set**. We report four representative tokenizers: SENT (flat baseline), H-SENT MC (hierarchical flat-walk), HDT MC (motif-community hierarchy), and HDTC (typed compositional). **Bold** = best among the four per dataset.

On **MOSES** (simple drug-like molecules), hierarchy provides modest but consistent improvements — SENT achieves 86.8% validity while HDT MC reaches 89.1% with the best FCD (2.30) and PGD (0.000). H-SENT MC achieves the best scaffold similarity (0.896). HDTC leads on motif rate (0.836). All four achieve near-identical motif-level fidelity (FG MMD ≤ 0.002). On **COCONUT** (complex natural products), the gap is dramatic: SENT drops to 58.0% validity while H-SENT MC reaches 92.0%, a **34-point improvement**. No single model dominates distributional metrics — HDT MC leads SNN (0.805), H-SENT MC leads scaffold similarity (0.090) and motif rate (0.696), while HDTC achieves the best FCD (4.74) and fragment similarity (0.969). HDT MC offers the best validity–novelty balance (91.0% validity, 96.7% novelty). The benefit of hierarchical tokenization scales with molecular complexity.

## Discussion

**Coarsening robustness depends on molecular complexity.** On MOSES (~20 atoms), generic coarsening methods fail catastrophically: HAC produces ≤21% validity for both H-SENT and HDT, and spectral coarsening fails for HDT (22%) while surviving for H-SENT (84%). Only chemistry-aware MC coarsening works reliably. On COCONUT (30–100 atoms), the picture reverses: all coarsening methods achieve 85–92% validity, with the MC-to-worst gap shrinking from ~68 points to ~7. Small molecules lack genuine multi-level structure, so topology-based algorithms produce chemically meaningless decompositions. Larger natural products contain real hierarchical motifs — ring systems, sugar moieties, terpene scaffolds — that even naive algorithms can discover.

**Hierarchy scales with molecular complexity.** On MOSES, SENT achieves 86.8% validity and HDT MC improves by only 2.3 points. On COCONUT, the gap widens to **34 points** (SENT 58.0% vs. H-SENT MC 92.0%). SENT's FG KL divergence of 0.523 on COCONUT (vs. 0.003 on MOSES) shows that flat walks produce completely wrong functional-group distributions on complex molecules. Hierarchy is not merely helpful but *necessary* for complex molecular generation.

**Validity–novelty tradeoff is structural.** More constrained tokenizers produce higher validity but lower novelty. On COCONUT, SENT achieves 100% novelty but only 58% validity; H-SENT MC reaches 92% validity but drops to 89.1% novelty; HDT MC achieves 91% validity while retaining 96.7% novelty. This tradeoff is inherent to the tokenizer architecture: hierarchical models decompose molecules into known motifs and learn to recombine them, constraining the output space. HDT MC offers the best balance, suggesting that edge-aware tokenization provides enough combinatorial freedom within valid chemistry to maintain diversity.

**H-SENT vs. HDT: global fidelity vs. local structure.** Within MC coarsening on COCONUT, HDT MC wins on global distribution metrics (FCD 13.17 vs. 14.95, SNN 0.805 vs. 0.781) while H-SENT MC wins on local structural recovery (scaffold similarity 0.090 vs. 0.040, motif rate 0.696 vs. 0.615). HDT's edge-aware tokenization helps the model learn correct global molecular fingerprints, but the additional edge tokens lengthen sequences and may reduce scaffold diversity. H-SENT's simpler bipartite partitioning produces shorter sequences, leaving more model capacity for scaffold-level variation.

**HDTC: composition trades diversity.** HDTC adds explicit type labels (Ring, Functional group, Scaffold) on top of edge-aware hierarchical tokenization. On COCONUT, this produces the strongest distributional match: FCD 4.74 (vs. next-best 12.77), SNN 0.990 (vs. 0.805), fragment similarity 0.969 (vs. 0.945), and the best motif rate (0.704). However, this comes at a steep diversity cost: HDTC has the lowest uniqueness (85.8%) and novelty (80.2%) of any model. On MOSES, the additional constraint offers diminishing returns: HDTC's validity (83.5%) is the lowest among the four summary models. Typed decomposition appears most valuable when the molecular space is complex enough that explicit structural roles provide genuine guidance.

**Full-reference vs. test-only evaluation reveals generalization gaps.** On COCONUT, SNN drops ~2× (0.71–0.81 → 0.39–0.44) and scaffold similarity drops ~3× when switching from full-reference to test-only evaluation, while fragment similarity and MMD metrics remain stable. HDTC exhibits the most extreme gap: SNN drops from 0.990 to 0.375 (2.6× reduction). This mirrors HDTC's low novelty: the model generates molecules near-identical to training examples but does not generalize to the held-out test set. With only 5,000 training molecules, all models fit the training distribution tightly, but more constrained tokenizers amplify this effect.

**Random walks as natural augmentation.** SENT re-samples a different random walk each time a molecule appears in a mini-batch, providing free data augmentation. With 1M molecules over 250K steps (batch size 16), the model sees ~16M total samples, each a distinct linearization. Hierarchical tokenizers produce deterministic sequences, reducing per-molecule diversity. This augmentation advantage helps SENT on simple molecules (strong MOSES baseline despite no structural bias) but is insufficient for complex molecules where compositional structure cannot be inferred from walk diversity alone.
